Roy Clay Sr.

Hewlette-Packard Silicon valley pioneer.

Here's a timeline of Roy Clay's professional career.

  • 1951 Roy recieved a degree in mathmatics from St. Louis University. His first job was a school teacher. Back then, teaching school was about the best job that African-Americans could reasonably hope to find in the U.S., and more than one of his early attempts to find work in the technology industry unfairly ended with comments like "Sorry Mr. Clay, but we have no jobs for professional Negroes."

  • 1956 Roy found his first job as a programmer at IBM andBurroughs computers in the McDonnell Aircraft Corporation, which later became McDonnell Douglass, a leading aircraft manufacturer at the time. Back then universities didn't offer Computer Science degrees so corporations often hired mathematics graduates for their software development positions instead.

  • 1958 Roy worked as a computer programmer for what is now Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory, a leading physics research lab iperated ny U.C. Berkley for the U.S. Department of energy, where he wrote software that demostrated how radiation particles would spread after above-ground nuclear explosions.

  • 1962 Roy Clay worked as a software engineer for Control Data Corporation, the third largest mainframe computer manufacturer at the time (behind IBM and Sperry Rand), where he developed software languages for Control Data computers.

  • 1965After an intense day and a half interview, Roy Clay then landed a position as the software development manager and lead developer for the HP 2116A minicomputer, which was the first computer sold by HP and only the second 16-bit computer, after the Honeywell DDP-116, to enter the world market. While there, Roy Clay worked tirelessly to make the software ready for the market as quickly as the hardware, which defied the industry convention at the time. Roy Clay also went on to become the first director of the HP Research and Development Computer Group, a relentless promoter for the development of Hewlett-Packardâ€™s computer division, and the interim General Manager of HP's computer division following the departure of Tom Perkins, who went on to co-found the iconic venture capital firm Kleiner Perkins Caufield & Byers. In addition, as HP's most senior African-American at the time, Roy Clay also helped a number of other minorities launch successful careers in the tech industry, such as industry notable Ken Coleman.

  • 1971 Roy Clay worked as a consultant that specialized in identifiying and nurturing prospective investments in computer technology start-ups for Kleiner Perkins Caufield & Byers, the premiere venture capital firm at the time. Three of those investments - Tandem Computers ($3B), Compaq ($22B), and Intel ($110B) - eventually attained a combined valuation of $135 billion dollars.

  • 1973 Roy Clay became the first non-Caucasian to serve on the Palo Alto City Council. He served as a councilman for Palo Alto, CA, from 1973 until 1979 and he served as the first African-American Vice Mayor of Palo Alto in 1976 and 1977.

  • 1977 Roy Clay founded ROD-L Electronics, the hipot and electrical-safety test equipment manufacturer that he continues to serve as CEO today. Manufacturers such as HP and GE use such testers to validate the electrical-safety and integrity of the line power devices that they produce, from computers to dishwashers.
